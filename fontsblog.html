This summer, I had the unique opportunity to study Dr. Julie Kientz at the University of Washington, in the department of Human Centered Design and Engineering. Dr. Kientz studies Human-Computer Interaction, Ubiquitous Computing, and Health Informatics and is passionate about the intersection of those fields with healthy living and learning. We conducted research on digital user experience to reduce the opportunity gap for low-to-middle income families and took a mixed methods approach. Over my ten week experience that was funded by the Computing Research Association, I helped conduct semi-structured initial and final interviews with 10 families and introduced digital voice assistant technology into their homes. As part of another project, I scraped, filtered, and analyzed relevant product reviews in a content analysis to understand perspectives and share design opportunities for voice assistants to support all families

Thus, as part of my Art 101 blog for this fall 2018 semester, I'd like to discuss a few of my interesting observations that I had this summer, both related and unrelated to my research. The first of which being the culture of recycling in Seattle.

Summer research at the University of Washington has given me the unique opportunity to spend 10 weeks in the progressive city of Seattle. So many news stories from the summer gave me hope for Mother Earth.

On July 1- Seattle became the first city to ban single use straws, stirrers and utensils. On July 9- Starbucks will ditch single use plastic straws in all cafes by 2020. And on July 17- Starbucks and Mcdonalds have joined forces to create a compostable cup.

On a hot Saturday afternoon, I sat at a burger joint near the receptacle area. Families stand near the receptacle area with a blank stare. One family was completely confused- I was too close to not provide some help. I explained how to properly dispose of everything from their meal. They thanked me for helping with a smile and sigh of relief. After a bit more research, I realized that even I MESSED UP. The Coca-Cola paper cup, that I thought was lined with plastic, is actually recyclable! See, it happens to the best of us. The confusion was shocking. I then proceeded to watch the process closely as four or five more tables got up to dispose of the scraps from their meal. One man grabbed items from his child and shoved them in the garbage in an “I don’t care”-type of fashion. Others assumed everything was recyclable and tossed everything, including compostable materials, into the large recycling can, an aspirational recycler, tossing it in out of sheer hope. Another family painstakingly stared at each product, each of the three bins and the restaurant's custom signage for each can, which led to success.

Next week, I plan to discuss next steps for recycling across America and specifically here in State College. Until then, think about the ways that you recycle?

Last week, I discussed my recycling concerns and hopes and now, I'd like to think about next steps for recycling across America and specifically here in State College.

So what's the next step, recycling education? Frequent articles by newspapers? required "look up" tools by town or city? Do you think it should be the responsibility of individual restaurants to create proper signage for customers to follow? Decrease in bin size to avoid an unsorted attitude? I'm not sure. But, what I am sure of is that other states and municipalities need to make this process simpler.

I moved into an apartment this year, off-campus. The State College recycling system is unreal. Everything must be separated and the apartment buildings have single cans outside of the buildings. So, you have to have the patience to separate everything or purchase several cans for the inside of your building that you would separate later on. This was absolutely insane. They have 3 cans for different colored glass! This process truly discourages students from recycling and encourages them to throw everything in the trash. Considering the immense college student population in State College, something needs to be done to replicate the Penn State recycling process here and make things more stream-lined.

Just as I began my semester blogging for this class with my concerns regarding recycling in State College vs. the changes in Seattle, I'd like to reflect on another experience that I had in Seattle.

This one, I re-visited two weeks ago when I returned to Seattle on a trip. It is quite possible that I might just be too used to the New York City metro system. But, I see an obvious need for growth to connect the various regions of Seattle to the north, south east and west, which are all connected by water. The homelessness crisis continues to exacerbate there and the dependency on busses with extreme traffic doesn’t reduce traffic. But, a more efficient metro system would improve travel conditions for all populations.

Currently, there seems to be too much accountability involved in the Seattle Light Rail design. There are two fare options: Orca card, physical ticket purchase and mobile application ticket purchase. There are no turnstiles to enter the subway system. Thus, there is no immediate requirement to pay in order to get on. It makes me uncomfortable that it’s basically the honor system that you have to pay to get on. They need this transportation system to grow and prosper. But, just two weeks ago, after spending ten weeks there this summer, was the first time that a transportation attendant checked my phone to ensure that I paid the fare, and scanned the Orca cards of the other riders. Now that I’ve introduced the problem, I'll discuss my attempts at a solution next week.

The Seattle public transportation system is expanding- north through North Seattle and East toward Bellevue, Washington, where Microsoft has many skyscrapers of offices, along with wealthy residents and many other large companies and firms. Currently, the project is underway moving in those directions and it is expected to be done in about a year and a half. It is interesting to me that the population influx over the past ten years thanks to companies like Amazon didn't push this to happen earlier.

Although the Seattle Light Rail system is expanding, I haven't noticed design changes for the actually stations. I wonder if the dozens of new stations will be designed to account for the lack of gatekeeping that requires payment, or if they will change the design of the existing stations to account for this. Currently, on the Light rail path which goes between the Seattle/Tacoma International Airport (from the south) and the University of Washington (north of downtown), there are several stops between the airport and downtown that are literally in the middle of the street. It would be near impossible to box it in to require payment. If they added turnstiles on the ends of the platforms, it would still be possible for citizens to jump onto the actual railway and avoid it.

But, in the existing stations that are underground and model metro stations across the country, there is definitely room for improvement. Currently, there are a half dozen opportunities to tap the payment card between the entrance to the station and entrance to the train. Also there are several pay stations to buy a ticket. But, nobody checks the ticket. So, in my opinion, i think they could add turnstiles at the top of the stairways and escalator entrances or even glass doors that slide in and out. I don’t think it would be worth it to make it extremely technical like the AmazonGo store because people follow the honor system already. Also, they could hire a few more attendants to check payment at the popular stations at the ends and the middle (i.e. University of Washington, Seatac Airport and Capitol Hill).

Going along with this design lens, I’d like to look at the processes in Starbucks. For example, starbucks payment has primarily shifted to digital. Customers who order with partners (baristas) at the counter are often paying with their Starbucks mobile app, in order to collect stars which is their reward mechanism. They also run other gamification strategies such as “Buy 3 teavana teas this weekend and earn an additional 30 bonus stars” or Holiday Bingo with many purchases. Baristas don’t push customers, in my personal experience, toward using the app. But it is much more efficient. I wonder: How many people mobile order in stores vs. non? I bet people regiously use one strategy or the next. I'm sure they have tons of data on this but are they interested in it.

College students are a huge US population that remains constant and is growing for Starbucks. How do they pay? Often, I see students using their debit card or a credit cards. Either they don’t know about the app or don’t care to find out. Will they be more loyal to Starbucks vs. Dunkin if they transition to paying with the app because of the ease of service and additional rewards and benefits?

Also, I wonder how the introduction of mobile order change sales at campus Starbucks. They would probably need 3 additional employees specifically making mobile order drinks. I wonder, exactly, how busy are these campus locations? In my personal experience, they seem constantly busy. As a possible experiment, maybe I could time it and count the thoroughfare. I wonder, is it worth it to add 3 $10/hour staff to keep up with the demand of mobile order and pay? I think so!
